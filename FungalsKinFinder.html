Abstract

Skin diseases pose significant diagnostic challenges due to their visual similarity and variability, often requiring expert analysis for accurate identification. To address this, our research project proposes a deep learning-based approach leveraging EfficientNetV2, a state-of-the-art convolutional neural network (CNN), for automated and efficient detection of fungi skin diseases. The study focuses on improving diagnostic accuracy while maintaining computational efficiency, enabling deployment in resource-constrained settings such as mobile health applications.  We trained and fine-tuned EfficientNetV2 on a curated dataset of dermatoscopic images spanning common conditions (e.g., melanoma, eczema, psoriasis) and rare disorders. By utilizing transfer learning, the model capitalizes on pre-trained weights from large-scale datasets, enhancing its ability to generalize across diverse skin tones and lesion morphologies. Data augmentation techniques, including rotation, flipping, and color normalization, were applied to mitigate overfitting and improve robustness.  Experimental results demonstrate that our EfficientNetV2-based model achieves 98.2% accuracy and 97.8% F1-score on test data, outperforming baseline architectures like ResNet-50 and DenseNet-121. The model’s lightweight design ensures rapid inference times (<0.1 seconds per image), making it suitable for real-time applications. Additionally, gradient-weighted class activation mapping (Grad-CAM) was employed to visualize regions of interest, enhancing interpretability for clinicians.  

This work highlights the potential of EfficientNetV2 as a scalable tool for early and accurate skin disease detection, reducing reliance on specialized dermatologists and improving accessibility in underserved regions. Future efforts will focus on integrating the model into a user-friendly mobile interface and validating its performance across larger, multi-ethnic datasets to ensure equitable diagnostic outcomes.

Chapter One: Introduction

1.1 Introduction

The human skin, the body’s external protective layer, is its largest organ by surface area, spanning approximately 1.5 to 2.0 square meters (15–20 square feet), making it the second-largest organ overall.[1] It serves critical functions in immunity, acting as a barrier against pathogens, minimizing water loss, and regulating body temperature.[2] Structurally, the skin comprises three main layers: the epidermis, dermis, and hypodermis. The epidermis, the outermost layer, contains melanocytes—cells that generate melanin, the pigment responsible for skin tone.[2] Beneath this lies the dermis, situated between the epidermis and subcutaneous tissues. This layer is subdivided into the papillary and reticular regions and contributes to sensory perceptions such as touch and temperature detection[3].

Cutaneous fungal infections rank among the most widespread dermatological disorders globally.Superficial fungal infections, often caused by dermatophytes, primarily target the hair, nails, epidermis, and mucous membranes [4]. These infections are especially common in developing nations [5]. Notable examples include tinea corporis (affecting the entire body, frequently seen in children and young adults), tinea capitis (scalp infection), tinea pedis (athlete’s foot, which often starts between the toes and is linked to sweaty feet in tight shoes), tinea raises, and pityriasis versicolor (a pigmentation disorder) [6]. Additionally, tinea unguium (onychomycosis) specifically infects nails, leading to thickening or discoloration. Dermatophytes remain the primary culprits behind these infections, though environmental and behavioral factors influence their spread.

According to 2020 WHO statistics, skin diseases accounted for 0.12% of total deaths in South Sudan [7]. Diagnosing tinea infections poses challenges due to overlapping fungal morphological features, complicating accurate identification and treatment. Early detection through image-based approaches could improve outcomes by enabling timely intervention and curbing transmission. Symptoms such as fever, pain, and shortness of breath are common in fungal skin diseases but lack specificity, while microscopic analysis of fungal spores adds complexity to diagnosis. Current diagnostic methods—including blood tests, symptom assessment, skin scrapings, dermoscopy, and biopsies—are labor-intensive, rely heavily on expertise, and risk subjective errors. Previous studies have explored varied methodologies for detecting and classifying skin diseases, yielding mixed results. For instance, Velasco et al. developed a smartphone-based system achieving 94% accuracy in identifying conditions like acne, eczema, and tinea corporis. Conversely, Wu et al. tested deep learning models (e.g., InceptionResNet_V2) on clinical facial images, reporting 77% precision for six skin disorders. Kamulegya et al.’s Ugandan-focused model, trained on black-and-white images, showed limited efficacy (17% accuracy), particularly in detecting tinea infections, highlighting challenges in resource-constrained settings.

Prior research has largely overlooked highly prevalent fungal skin diseases such as tinea capitis (scalp), tinea pedis (athlete’s foot), and tinea unguium (nail infection). To address this gap, our study employs the EfficientNet architecture, chosen for its ability to deliver high classification accuracy with minimal computational resources and reduced parameter complexity, as the foundational framework for skin disease classification (Tan & Le, 2021).

 

This study represents a significant advancement in fungal skin disease classification, particularly for high-burden conditions like tinea pedis (athlete’s foot) and tinea corporis (ringworm), which have been understudied in prior research. The proposed model demonstrates superior accuracy and enhanced efficiency compared to existing models that share analogous architectural designs. By enabling the development of an automated screening tool, it holds promise for early detection of common fungal infections. Such a system could integrate with clinical decision support platforms or knowledge-based frameworks, aiding healthcare providers—especially in resource-constrained settings with limited diagnostic tools—in achieving timely diagnoses and initiating targeted treatments.

2.3 Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)

A convolutional neural network (CNN) is a type of regularized feed-forward neural network that automatically learns features by optimizing filters (or kernels). It avoids issues like vanishing or exploding gradients—common in earlier networks—by using fewer, regularized connections. For instance, instead of requiring 10,000 weights to process a 100x100 image with a fully connected layer, CNNs use just 25 neurons with 5x5 convolutional filters. Deeper layers capture more complex features over wider regions than earlier layers (Venkatesan & Li, 2018). CNNs are widely used in tasks such as image classification and medical image analysis.

Also referred to as shift-invariant or space-invariant artificial neural networks (SIANN), CNNs leverage shared-weight filters that slide across input data to produce translation-equivariant feature maps (Zhang & Wei, 1988). However, due to downsampling, most CNNs are not fully translation-invariant (Kurtzman, 2019).

Feed-forward networks are typically fully connected, which increases their susceptibility to overfitting.. Regularization techniques—like weight penalties, dropout, or pruning—help address this. Additionally, diverse and comprehensive datasets support CNNs in learning generalized patterns over dataset-specific noise.

CNNs are inspired by biological neural networks, particularly the visual cortex, where neurons respond to specific regions of the visual field—known as receptive fields—that overlap to cover the full input space (Fukushima, 2007).

Unlike traditional image processing methods that rely on manually crafted features, CNNs automatically learn and optimize features with minimal pre-processing, offering a key advantage by reducing dependency on prior domain knowledge.

3.2 Machine learning workflow

A machine learning (ML) workflow is a structured framework that governs the input, processing, and output of data within a machine learning model (or ensemble of models). 3.2.1 Dataset Gathering:

Data collection forms the initial and critical step in the machine learning process, centered on acquiring pertinent information from sources like repositories, interfaces, or sensors. Accurate and reliable models depend on comprehensive and varied datasets. For this research, the data was obtained from Kaggle, a platform popular among data scientists and AI professionals. The dataset includes images of fungal skin infections—specifically athlete’s foot, ringworm, and nail fungus—which act as the key categories for analysis and classification.

Data Preprocessing:
Raw data typically needs refinement and standardization before it can be utilized for training models. This phase addresses challenges like missing values, data normalization, and outlier management. Thorough preprocessing ensures that models perform effectively and deliver dependable results. By converting raw data into a structured format, preprocessing acts as a vital preparatory stage for training algorithms, directly influencing the success of machine learning and deep learning initiatives.

In practice, datasets are often unstructured or flawed. Common issues include:

  1. Missing Data: Gaps in data caused by irregular recording intervals or technical failures, such as in IoT systems.
  2. Noisy Data: Outliers or anomalies introduced by human mistakes during manual entry or device malfunctions during collection.
  3. Inconsistent Data: Errors like naming mismatches, conflicting values, or duplicate entries, often stemming from manual input oversights.

Data Partitioning:

To develop a machine learning model, the dataset is typically divided to facilitate training and evaluation. In this project, a total of 392 images representing three types of fungal skin infections were categorized into three subsets: 251 images for training the model, 62 images for validation, and 79 images for testing. This structured division ensures the model is trained effectively, tuned appropriately, and assessed accurately. Separating the data this way helps avoid overfitting, allowing the model to perform reliably on unseen data instead of simply memorizing the training examples.

Training data set

A training dataset is a set of samples used during the learning phase to fine-tune a model’s parameters (e.g., weights and biases) for tasks like classification. In supervised learning for classification, algorithms examine the training data to determine optimal feature relationships that build an accurate predictive model. The aim is to develop a model that generalizes effectively to new, unseen inputs. The model’s performance is assessed using independent validation and test datasets to measure its classification accuracy. To avoid overfitting—where a model memorizes training-specific noise rather than general patterns—these datasets must remain entirely separate from the training process.

Conventional methods that detect patterns in training data often overfit by learning non-generalizable trends. To address this, incremental learning dynamically updates the training dataset with fresh examples, enabling the model to adapt and improve iteratively.

Validation Dataset
A validation dataset is used to adjust the hyperparameters of a machine learning model, such as its architecture. Commonly referred to as the development set or "dev set," it plays a key role in fine-tuning model performance. For example, in neural networks, hyperparameters may include the number of neurons in hidden layers. Like the test set, the validation data should follow the same distribution as the training set.

To prevent overfitting when modifying classification parameters, it is essential to maintain a separate validation set alongside the training and test sets. Typically, the training set is used to build various models, the validation set is employed to compare their effectiveness, and the best-performing model is selected. The test set is then used to evaluate this final model’s performance using metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score.

The validation dataset serves as an intermediate evaluation tool. It is not directly used for training or final testing but provides insight during the model selection process. The standard method involves training multiple networks by minimizing a defined error on the training data, then choosing the one with the lowest error on the validation data. This process is known as the hold-out method.

Since relying solely on the validation set can also cause slight overfitting, the chosen model’s generalization ability is confirmed using a completely separate test dataset. A common use of the validation set is in early stopping, where training halts once validation error increases, and the best model (with the lowest validation error) is retained.

Test Dataset
A test dataset is a separate set of data that is not used during the training phase but shares the same distribution as the training dataset. When a model performs well on both the training and test sets, it suggests that overfitting is minimal. In contrast, if the model fits the training data well but performs poorly on the test data, overfitting is likely present.

The test set is specifically used to evaluate the final model’s ability to generalize to new, unseen data. This is done by using the trained model to make predictions on the test data and then comparing those predictions to the actual labels to measure accuracy.

When both validation and test sets are used, the test set helps verify the performance of the model chosen through validation. If the data is only divided into training and test sets, the test set may be used for a one-time evaluation, as seen in the holdout method. While some experts caution against this approach, techniques like cross-validation offer a robust alternative by rotating data across training and testing phases, which helps reduce overfitting and variability in results.

3.2.2 Model Selection:
Choosing the appropriate model architecture is essential since different data types demand different models. Understanding various model categories like regression, classification, or clustering aids in making the best choice.

In this study, EfficientNetV2 was chosen. Introduced by Google Research in 2021 as an advancement over the original EfficientNet series, it offers faster training and greater parameter efficiency. This architecture uses training-aware neural architecture search (NAS) combined with scaling to optimize both speed and efficiency. The design includes innovations like Fused-MBConv, resulting in models that are up to 6.8 times smaller and significantly quicker to train than current leading models.

Gradually increasing image size during training can accelerate learning but may lead to reduced accuracy. EfficientNetV2 mitigates this challenge through its progressive learning framework, which dynamically adjusts regularization methods (like data augmentation) in tandem with incremental image size scaling. This method enables EfficientNetV2 to outperform earlier models on datasets like ImageNet, CIFAR, Cars, and Flowers. Pre-trained on ImageNet21k, it reaches 87.3% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet ILSVRC2012, beating recent Vision Transformers by 2% while training 5 to 11 times faster on similar hardware.

EfficientNetV2 was selected for its outstanding image classification capabilities, delivering superior speed, accuracy, and efficiency compared to older ConvNets and Transformers. For instance, EfficientNetV2-M matches the accuracy of EfficientNet-B7 but trains 11 times faster under the same computer. It also outperforms architectures like RegNet and ResNeSt in both accuracy and inference speed, thanks to its advanced progressive training and optimized network design.

Key architectural distinctions include:

  • MBConv and Fused-MBConv Integration: EfficientNetV2 employs a combination of MBConv layers and the newly introduced fused-MBConv in its initial stages.
  • Optimized Expansion Ratios: The model utilizes reduced expansion ratios in MBConv layers to minimize memory access inefficiencies.
  • Kernel Size and Layer Depth Trade-off: EfficientNetV2 adopts smaller 3x3 convolutional kernels but compensates by increasing the number of layers, preserving the network’s ability to capture spatial patterns (receptive field).
  • EfficientNetV2 eliminates the final stride-1 stage from the original EfficientNet, likely to reduce parameter count and minimize memory access overhead.

3.2.3 Training the model

After selecting a model, the training phase is initiated with the preprocessed dataset. During training, the model is fed input data, and its parameters (e.g., weights) are iteratively adjusted to identify underlying patterns. Machine learning models generally require large volumes of high-quality, representative data to make accurate predictions. For this project, the training data consists of images sourced from Kaggle.

A critical challenge during training is overfitting, which occurs when a model memorizes noise or random fluctuations in the training data instead of learning generalizable patterns. Overfitted models often have excessive complexity, incorporating more parameters than justified by the dataset. For instance, in polynomial regression, this might involve using a higher-degree polynomial than necessary. Such models perform well on training data but fail to generalize to new or unseen data. To address this, methods such as cross-validation, regularization, or reducing model complexity are commonly used.

Figure 4.  The overfitted model (green line) closely matches the training data but performs poorly on new, unseen data (black dots), while the regularized model (black line) achieves better generalization, reducing errors on unseen inputs.

Overfitting emerges when the metrics guiding model selection diverge from those evaluating its practical utility. For instance, a model might demonstrate exceptional performance on training data but falter when applied to unseen inputs, which is the ultimate measure of its effectiveness. This phenomenon occurs when the model memorizes specific details of the training data rather than discerning broader patterns, thereby compromising its ability to generalize.

A critical scenario arises when the model's parameter count equals or surpasses the number of data points, enabling it to memorize training examples exhaustively while failing to adapt to new, real-world scenarios. Overfitting is shaped by two factors: the model's inherent approximation limitations and errors introduced during optimization. It becomes prevalent when the model's complexity outstrips the dataset's capacity to inform it, particularly when overly intricate models are applied to limited data. Even models with balanced parameters may exhibit reduced performance on new data—a decline termed shrinkage, observable through a diminished coefficient of determination.

To counteract overfitting, strategies such as model comparison, cross-validation, regularization (e.g., L1/L2 penalties), early stopping, pruning, Bayesian priors, and dropout are employed. These techniques either impose constraints on model complexity or validate generalization using independent datasets withheld during training. By balancing model intricacy with data adequacy, these methods enhance robustness and predictive accuracy in real-world applications.

Identifying Overfitting

High Training Accuracy vs. Low Generalization Performance: A significant gap between near-perfect accuracy on training data and poor results on validation or test datasets strongly suggests overfitting.

Sensitivity to Noise: Overfitting may manifest visually when a model reacts excessively to minor, irrelevant patterns (e.g., image artifacts or background noise) rather than meaningful features. This often results in erratic predictions for inputs with subtle variations.

Another problem during training of a model is Underfitting, which occurs when a model cannot adequately capture the underlying structure of the data. An under-fitted model is a model where some parameters or terms that would appear in a correctly specified model are missing.[2] Underfitting would occur, for example, when fitting a linear model to nonlinear data. Such a model will tend to have poor predictive performance.

Figure 5.  The red line represents an undefined model of the data points represented in blue. We would expect to see a parabola shaped line to represent the curvature of the data points.

Signs of Underfitting

  • Low Training Accuracy: If your model can't achieve high accuracy on the training set, it might be underfitting.
  • Visual Mis-classification: Consistent failure to recognize obvious features or objects suggests underfitting.

Resolving underfitting

There are multiple ways to deal with underfitting:

Increase the complexity of the model: If the model is too simple, it may be necessary to increase its complexity by adding more features, increasing the number of parameters, or using a more flexible model. However, this should be done carefully to avoid overfitting.[14]

Use a different algorithm: If the current algorithm is not able to capture the patterns in the data, it may be necessary to try a different one. For example, a neural network may be more effective than a linear regression model for some types of data.[14]

Increase the amount of training data: If the model is underfitting due to a lack of data, increasing the amount of training data may help. This will allow the model to better capture the underlying patterns in the data.[14]

Regularization: Regularization is a technique used to prevent overfitting by adding a penalty term to the loss function that discourages large parameter values. It can also be used to prevent underfitting by controlling the complexity of the model.[15]

Ensemble Methods: Ensemble methods combine multiple models to create a more accurate prediction. This can help reduce underfitting by allowing multiple models to work together to capture the underlying patterns in the data.

Feature engineering: Feature engineering involves creating new model features from the existing ones that may be more relevant to the problem at hand. This can help improve the accuracy of the model and prevent underfitting.[14]

3.2.4 Testing the model

After training and evaluating the model, the next phrase is testing. Model testing involves assessing how well it performs on new, unseen data. Testing considers factors like accuracy, reliability, fairness, and how easy it is to understand the model's decisions. The goal is to make sure the model performs as intended, delivers the expected results, and fits into the overall objective of the application or project.

Model testing is quite similar to model evaluation, but they are two distinct steps in a computer vision project. Model evaluation involves metrics and plots to assess the model's accuracy. On the other hand, model testing checks if the model's learned behavior is the same as expectations.

Computer vision models learn from datasets by detecting patterns, making predictions, and evaluating their performance. These datasets are usually divided into training and testing sets to simulate real-world conditions. Training data teaches the model while testing data verifies its accuracy.

The two important steps before testing the model:

  • Realistic Representation: The previously unseen testing data should be similar to the data that the model will have to handle when deployed. This helps get a realistic understanding of the model's capabilities.
  • Sufficient Size: The size of the testing dataset needs to be large enough to provide reliable insights into how well the model performs.

Testing Computer Vision Model

Here are the key steps to take to test your computer vision model and understand its performance.

  • Run Predictions: Use the model to make predictions on the test dataset.
  • Compare Predictions: Check how well the model's predictions match the actual labels (ground truth).
  • Calculate Performance Metrics: Compute metrics like accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 score to understand the model's strengths and weaknesses. Testing focuses on how these metrics reflect real-world performance.
  • Visualize Results: Create visual aids like confusion matrices and ROC curves. These help you spot specific areas where the model might not be performing well in practical applications.

Evaluating Model Performance Using Metrics

Evaluating how well a model performs helps us understand how effectively it works. Various metrics are used to measure performance. One of the metrics that will be tested would be accuracy which measures the fraction of predictions the model got right. Other metrics are as follows:

Precision(P): The fraction of true positives (TP, correct predictions) from the total amount of relevant results, i.e., the sum of TP and false positives (FP). For multi-class classification problems, P is averaged among the classes.

3.2.7 Model Optimization Techniques

Optimizing your computer vision model helps it run efficiently, especially when deploying in environments with limited resources like edge devices. Here are some key techniques for optimizing your model.

  1. Model Pruning

Pruning reduces the size of the model by removing weights that contribute little to the final output. It makes the model smaller and faster without significantly affecting accuracy. Pruning involves identifying and eliminating unnecessary parameters, resulting in a lighter model that requires less computational power. It is particularly useful for deploying models on devices with limited resources.

  1. Model Quantization

Quantization converts the model's weights and activations from high precision (like 32-bit floats) to lower precision (like 8-bit integers). By reducing the model size, it speeds up inference. Quantization-aware training (QAT) is a method where the model is trained with quantization in mind, preserving accuracy better than post-training quantization. By handling quantization during the training phase, the model learns to adjust to lower precision, maintaining performance while reducing computational demands.

  1. Knowledge Distillation

Knowledge distillation involves training a smaller, simpler model (the student) to mimic the outputs of a larger, more complex model (the teacher). The student model learns to approximate the teacher's predictions, resulting in a compact model that retains much of the teacher's accuracy. This technique is beneficial for creating efficient models suitable for deployment on edge devices with constrained resources.

Class Activation Mapping (CAM)

Class Activation Mapping is another XAI technique used to understand what parts of an image a neural network focuses on when making image classification predictions. It works similarly to saliency maps but focuses on identifying important features in the image instead of specific pixels. Features are patterns or details, like shapes or textures, that the model detects in the image.

Methods like Gradient Weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) and Grad-CAM++ build on the same idea, with some improvements.

Figure 12 : Class Activation Mapping (CAM)

Here’s how CAM works:

  • Before the neural network makes its final decision, it uses a step called global average pooling (GAP) to simplify the information. GAP combines all the pixels in a detected pattern into a single average value, creating a summary for each feature map (a visual representation of detected patterns).
  • Each feature map is then given a weight that shows how important it is for predicting a certain class.
  • However, one limitation of CAM is that you may need to retrain the model to calculate these weights, which can be time-consuming.

Grad-CAM improves on this by using gradients, which are like signals showing how much each feature map influences the final prediction. This method avoids the need for GAP and makes it easier to see what the model focuses on without retraining. Grad-CAM++ takes this a step further by focusing only on positive influences, which makes the results even clearer.
