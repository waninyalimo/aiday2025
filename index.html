Abstract

Skin diseases pose significant diagnostic challenges due to their visual similarity and variability, often requiring expert analysis for accurate identification. To address this, our research project proposes a deep learning-based approach leveraging EfficientNetV2, a state-of-the-art convolutional neural network (CNN), for automated and efficient detection of fungi skin diseases. The study focuses on improving diagnostic accuracy while maintaining computational efficiency, enabling deployment in resource-constrained settings such as mobile health applications.  We trained and fine-tuned EfficientNetV2 on a curated dataset of dermatoscopic images spanning common conditions (e.g., Ringworm, Athlete foot, Nail fungus) and rare disorders. By utilizing transfer learning, the model capitalizes on pre-trained weights from large-scale datasets, enhancing its ability to generalize across diverse skin tones and lesion morphologies.Data augmentation techniques, including rotation, flipping, and color normalization, were applied to mitigate overfitting and improve robustness.  Experimental results demonstrate that our EfficientNetV2-based model achieves 84.2% accuracy. The model’s lightweight design ensures rapid inference times (<0.1 seconds per image), making it suitable for real-time applications. Additionally, gradient-weighted class activation mapping (Grad-CAM) was employed to visualize regions of interest, enhancing interpretability for clinicians.  

This work highlights the potential of EfficientNetV2 as a scalable tool for early and accurate skin disease detection, reducing reliance on specialized dermatologists and improving accessibility in underserved regions. Future efforts will focus on integrating the model into a user-friendly mobile interface and validating its performance across larger, multi-ethnic datasets to ensure equitable diagnostic outcomes.

Method Overview

Figure 1: An efficientNet model extracts patterns from a fungal skin image and classifies into one of the three categories.

The identification of various fungal skin diseases was achieved by employing EfficientNet v2 to extract subtle patterns from images. Our model was trained on a specialized dataset, predominantly sourced from Kaggle, which included images representing athlete's foot, nail fungi, and ringworm. Trained the model for 100 epochs, achieving a test accuracy of 96.5% and an F1-score of 95.8%.
